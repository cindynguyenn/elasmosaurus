History of Elasmosaurus: How it was Discovered and Named

Elasmosaurus is an extinct marine reptile that lived during the Late Cretaceous period, approximately 80 million years ago. The first fossil remains of Elasmosaurus were discovered in 1868 by the famous American paleontologist Edward Drinker Cope.

Cope initially misinterpreted the long neck of Elasmosaurus, mistaking it for a tail, and incorrectly placed the head at the end of what he thought was the tail. This mistake led to a controversy with another paleontologist, Othniel C. Marsh, who had also discovered a similar fossil, but correctly identified the head at the other end of the long neck. The controversy, known as the "Bone Wars," lasted for several years and became infamous in the history of paleontology.

Eventually, it was recognized that Elasmosaurus had an extremely long neck, consisting of 72 vertebrae, and a relatively small head with sharp teeth for catching fish and squid. Elasmosaurus likely swam using its four flippers, which were similar in shape and function to those of modern sea turtles.

Since the “Bone Wars,” several more Elasmosaurus fossils have been found throughout North America, particularly in the western United States, including Montana, South Dakota, Wyoming, and Kansas. It is believed that Elasmosaurus was a top predator in the Late Cretaceous oceans, feeding on small fish and other aquatic animals.

Elasmosaurus was a large marine reptile with a unique and distinctive appearance. It had an extremely long neck consisting of 72 vertebrae, which made up more than half of its total body length. Some scientists also believe that Elasmosaurus may have had a pouch in its throat, which it could fill with air to help regulate its buoyancy while swimming.

While Elasmosaurus was suited for aquatic life, it was certainly restricted. Based on its body shape and biomechanics of its swimming style, it is thought to have been a relatively slow swimmer. It used its four flippers to propel itself through the water in a slow and lumbering manner, rather the efficient motion used by dolphins and sharks. It also needed to keep its body totally immersed in the water in order for the water to support its body weight. Moreover, a longer neck meant more body tissue, which resulted in more weight. More weight means that stronger muscles would be needed in order to support the neck if it were to be out of the water. Studies have shown Elasmosaurus did not have the muscles to move its neck when it did not have water to support the weight. Overall, the physical characteristics of Elasmosaurus were well-suited to its life as a top predator in the Late Cretaceous oceans. Its long neck and streamlined body allowed it to swim quickly and efficiently, while its sharp teeth and powerful flippers helped it catch and devour its prey.
